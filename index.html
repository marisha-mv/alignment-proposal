01 · Where we are i / x

Last quarter we ran 600 projects.
Most of them, no one watched.

Mindvalley is a $108M company at 9% EBITDA. To grow EBITDA we don't need more projects — we need fewer of them, executed by the right people. Last quarter's data says we are doing the opposite. 600 projects sounds like productivity. It is sprawl. Forty percent of people never reported into OKRs at all — we don't know what they did. Of the OKRs we could see, seventy percent failed. The healthy failure rate for stretch OKRs is roughly 30%; ours is more than double. That is not ambition. That is misalignment.

The pattern that costs us focus
Six hundred projects, forty percent invisible, seventy percent failing. This is not an execution problem on any single team — it is a structural problem with the operating model. We are running a $108M business with the project discipline of a startup that hasn't yet decided what it is. Until we cut that down to a list we can actually run, no individual team's effort will compound into company-level results.
02 · The thesis ii / x

EBITDA grows by concentration,
not by activity.

A 9% EBITDA business at $108M cannot grow margin by adding more work. It grows margin by removing work that doesn't move the needle, and concentrating capacity on work that does. That is the entire reason for this proposal. Not a re-org. Not a re-brand. A re-allocation of where Mindvalley's people spend their week.

1

Cut unnecessary projects.

If a project doesn't directly support one of the 14, it is a candidate for elimination — not deferral, not "next quarter," not "we'll get to it." Cut. The default disposition for an unmapped project is stopped this week, with the burden of proof on keeping it.

2

Concentrate the people we have.

The non-supporting work is our redeploy pool. Most "we don't have capacity" complaints in this company are not capacity problems — they are misallocation problems. Once we know who is currently spending their week on work that doesn't move the 14, we can move them to the work that does.

3

Identify the core team.

By the end of this audit, we will know — by name — who is on the critical path of the 14, who enables them, and who is doing work the company does not need right now. That answers the harder structural questions about cost, capacity, and EBITDA. We can't make those decisions until we can see the picture.

The test, every project, every person, every meeting
Does this move one of the 14 forward? If yes — name how, name by when. If no — it is either a candidate for cut or a candidate for redeploy. There is no third answer.

04 · What success looks like iv / x

By the end of this initiative,
we know three things.

1

The core projects we're driving — to move cash to bank.

The 14 priorities, ratified by execs, are the company's core projects this quarter. They are what move cash to bank. Every other project across the company either supports one of the 14 — or it is a candidate for cut. One canonical list. One direction.

2

The core team pushing those projects forward.

Named. Per priority, who's doing what. The contributors on the 14 are the people pushing the major initiatives forward — and by the end of the audit, every one of them is clear on what their work delivers to cash and bank, in dollars, not abstractions. Shubham knows that Manifesting Pathway launching by Jun 30 means a specific number landing in the account.

3

The redeploy pool — moved to cash-generating work no one is doing today.

Everyone not on the core projects is the redeploy pool. They get moved onto cash-generating initiatives that aren't getting any attention right now — affiliates ($1M revenue we're not earning), community ($1.5M in refunds we're not preventing), B2B (no one selling AI Mastery to B2B today) — and other opportunities the executive team identifies. These are dollars sitting on the table because no one currently owns them.

The output, in one sentence
The core team driving the 14 — and the redeploy pool funding the cash-generating work no one is doing today.
05 · How it works v / x

How — each exec runs it
for their team.

Each exec runs the audit for their division. Same four steps for everyone, two-week timeline. At the end, one OKR review where the executive team confirms everyone is on the right things. After that, ongoing OKR management is each exec's own job.

1

Map your team's projects and sub-projects to the core projects. Week 1

Most teams are supporting teams — your projects and sub-projects have to map upward to one of the core projects they support. If something doesn't map, it's a candidate for cut. Share the map with your whole team so everyone sees how their work connects.

2

Name the core team, the adjacent team, and the redeployed team. Week 1

Core team: named owner of a priority sub-project, end to end. Adjacent team: enabling work the core projects depend on. Redeployed team: neither. Name each person. If someone is nominally on a priority but not owning a sub-project, they're floating — they go to the redeployed team.

3

Make a list of the redeployed team and bring it to the executives. Week 2

You don't redistribute alone. Bring the redeploy list — names, current work, possible moves — to the executive team. We decide together where each person goes.

4

One OKR review — confirm everyone is on the right things. End of Week 2

After the audit, the executive team holds one OKR review together. Each exec walks through their team's OKRs — confirming every person is owning the right sub-project, supporting the right priority, or properly redeployed. Going forward, ongoing OKR management is each exec's job for their own team. The collective review happens once — to verify alignment.

Each person owns one sub-project — and is accountable
Task: Pavel writes emails for the 90-day journey.
Ownership: Pavel owns 90-day LTV — for members and non-members. He looks at the entire 90-day journey: how people are onboarded, what they're upsold (accelerator, full membership, other products), how to get them to purchase the right thing at the right time. His job is to grow 90-day LTV. The metric is his.

That is the bar. One owner per sub-project — named, accountable, end to end.
06 · The result vi / x

The result: the core team —
and where redeploy goes.

When every exec finishes the four steps, three columns exist company-wide. Two are about how the 14 actually ship. The third is concrete dollars Mindvalley is leaving on the table because no one currently owns it.

Column 1 · The engine

Core

  • Definition: owns one of the priority sub-projects end to end — accountable for strategy, execution, and results.
  • Investment: protect, unblock, do not interrupt with non-priority work.
  • Watch for: single points of failure (e.g., Janja owning 3 summits).
  • Outcome: the names that must be present for Q2 to ship.
Column 2 · The floor

Adjacent

  • Definition: enabling work the 14 depends on (legal, IT, finance ops, infra).
  • Investment: maintain, optimize, automate where possible.
  • Watch for: "we serve everyone" claims that aren't verified by recipients.
  • Outcome: the named functions Mindvalley needs to keep the lights on.
Column 3 · The open question

Redeploy

  • Definition: <20% of week on the 14, no enabling tie.
  • Investment: a decision in 30 days — move them or have a different conversation.
  • Watch for: protecting individuals through ambiguity. Name the work, not the person.
  • First destinations: affiliates, community, B2B — examples below.

07 · The rhythm vii / x

The rhythm: Daily Huddle,
Tuesday and Thursday in office.

Once the core team is named, naming isn't enough. The contributors on the 14 need to operate as one execution machine — in daily contact, in the same room twice a week. The rhythm IS the integration.

1

Contributors report in the Daily Huddle.

The Daily Huddle is already where tactical information moves at Mindvalley — and a lot is shared. The change is small but specific: every contributor on the 14 attends, and the priority projects get an update from the person doing the work, not a layer up. If Shubham owns Manifesting Pathway, Shubham reports. The Daily Huddle becomes the live, daily picture of where the 14 stand — visible to the whole company.

2

Office presence — Tuesday and Thursday.

Contributors come to the office Tuesday and Thursday, every week. Pathway launches need Product in the room with Marketing. Summits need Academy in the room with Events. Not because remote doesn't work generally — because the 14 require cross-team coordination that doesn't happen well in DMs. Two days a week, in person, together — and that's how culture gets built around the work.

Why the rhythm matters
The 14 are not fourteen separate projects — they are one execution machine with cross-dependencies. Pathway launches need the .com revamp. Summits feed Mastery launches. Without daily contact among contributors, those dependencies surface as missed deadlines instead of unblock requests. The Daily Huddle catches the dependency early. Tuesday and Thursday lets us close it in person — and builds the camaraderie that makes the team want to ship together.

09 · What changes this week ix / x

Four asks of this room.

1

Ratify the 14 — and one named owner per sub-project, end to end.

The 14 are the canonical list. Under each, subprojects that have to ship — accelerators, upsells, content, ops. Every sub-project gets one named owner — accountable for strategy, execution, and results. Not contributing. Not doing one piece. Owning. The owner is the person who wakes up thinking about that sub-project, identifies what's broken, proposes the fix, and drives it through to results. The 14 stays canonical; subprojects flow underneath; each has a name next to it.

2

Run the two-week audit for your team.

By end of Week 1: projects + sub-projects mapped to the core projects (shared with your whole team), and your team named (core / adjacent / redeployed). By end of Week 2: the redeployed list brought to the executive team — we decide redistribution together. Manager-attested. Default for any non-submission = "Cut." No exceptions.

3

One OKR review — to confirm everyone is on the right things.

After the two-week audit, the executive team holds one OKR review together. Each exec walks through their team's OKRs to confirm every person is working on the right things — owning a core sub-project, supporting one, or properly redeployed. Going forward, ongoing OKR management is each exec's responsibility for their own team. We do this once, to verify alignment.

4

Your core team is in the Daily Huddle — and in the office Tuesday and Thursday.

Contributors on the 14 attend the Daily Huddle and report on their projects directly. They come to the office Tuesday and Thursday, every week. Camaraderie is built in person; the 14 ship faster when the people on them know each other.
